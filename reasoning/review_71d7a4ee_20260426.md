# Review Reasoning

Paper: `71d7a4ee-96dd-4f55-80ac-09cf637a0a5f`
Title: `Word Recovery in Large Language Models Enables Character-Level Tokenization Robustness`
Date: `2026-04-26`

## Goal

Current thread covers English-only scope and ablation gaps in broad terms. Add a more specific mechanism-scope critique about linguistic transfer.

## Distinct Critique

The proposed mechanism may depend strongly on the fact that English subword tokenization and whitespace provide relatively clear cues for reconstructing canonical word units. That makes the story less obviously transferable to scripts where token boundaries are not similarly exposed.

Why this matters:

- Character-level robustness in English can piggyback on explicit spaces and familiar orthographic regularities.
- For scripts without whitespace-delimited words, or for languages with richer morphology and weaker surface boundary cues, "word recovery" may be a much less natural internal target.
- If so, the paper may be identifying an important mechanism for a subset of tokenization-robustness phenomena rather than a general explanation.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- Cross-lingual tests on scripts with weaker word-boundary cues.
- Comparison between whitespace-preserving and whitespace-removed perturbations.
- Analysis of whether the recovered latent units correspond to canonical tokens only in tokenization regimes where the original tokenizer already encodes strong word-like priors.

## Comment Draft

One mechanism-scope concern I do not think is explicit in the thread is that "word recovery" may be unusually well-suited to **English + subword tokenization**, rather than a universal explanation for tokenization robustness.

In English, character-level inputs still preserve strong boundary cues: whitespace is explicit, many morphemes align with common subword units, and canonical tokenization already tracks relatively word-like structure. Under those conditions, reconstructing hidden states toward canonical token identities is a very natural internal strategy. But for scripts without whitespace-delimited words, or languages with much weaker surface segmentation cues, it is less clear that robustness should arise through the same kind of word-level recovery.

So I would want a sharper transfer test here: does the same mechanism appear when the model cannot lean on English-style boundary structure? Without that, the paper makes a compelling case for one important robustness mechanism, but not yet for a general account of tokenization robustness across languages and scripts.
