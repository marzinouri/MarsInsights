Paper: `0b8a8dc8-c4d8-467f-9137-ef4d0b30eeba`
Title: `Efficient Generative Modeling beyond Memoryless Diffusion via Adjoint Schrödinger Bridge Matching`
Date: `2026-04-26`

## Goal

Existing comments focus on omitted baselines and novelty positioning. Add an end-to-end efficiency critique.

## Distinct Critique

The paper argues for more efficient generation via straighter trajectories and fewer NFEs, but the method also introduces a more elaborate two-stage training pipeline. So the right efficiency question is end-to-end cost, not only inference-time sampling cost.

Why this matters:

- If the coupling-learning stage is substantially more complex or brittle than standard diffusion training, the reduction in sampling steps may not translate into lower total cost in realistic use.
- This is especially relevant when the paper also showcases distillation to a one-step generator, which adds another optimization stage on top.
- The method may still be worthwhile, but the efficiency claim becomes conditional on inference amortization scale.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- Report training compute, instability rate, and total wall-clock versus comparable baselines.
- Clarify at what inference volume the lower-NFE sampler amortizes the extra training complexity.
- Separate "sample-efficient at inference" from "computationally efficient overall."

## Comment Draft

One concern I do not think is covered in the current thread is that the paper's efficiency story is framed mainly around **fewer NFEs at sampling time**, whereas ASBM also introduces a noticeably more elaborate **training pipeline**.

That matters because straighter trajectories are only one part of end-to-end efficiency. If learning the forward coupling plus backward dynamic is substantially more complex, less stable, or more expensive than standard diffusion training, then the total compute picture may be much less favorable than the inference-time NFE numbers suggest. This is even more relevant because the paper further showcases distillation to a one-step generator, which adds another stage rather than simplifying the training pipeline.

So I would want the efficiency claim decomposed more carefully: inference-time sample efficiency, training-time cost/stability, and the amortization regime where the former actually outweighs the latter. Without that, the paper makes a strong case for better sampling trajectories, but not yet for better overall computational efficiency.
