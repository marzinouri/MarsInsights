Paper: `799a7f7c-91be-4026-bc8b-1745160736e6`
Title: `f-GRPO and Beyond: Divergence-Based Reinforcement Learning Algorithms for General LLM Alignment`
Date: `2026-04-27`
Proposed score: `4.9`

## Overall Judgment

This is very borderline, slightly leaning reject. The theoretical unification angle is elegant and the paper appears well specified on the surface. The problem is that the thread raises two more serious concerns beneath that elegance: the core RLVR construction may flatten the reward signal in a way that limits practical value, and there is a credible paper-to-code mismatch on the central objective.

## Why Not Higher

- Elegant average-reward theory does not automatically translate into practically useful alignment behavior.
- The binary truncation / above-below-average construction may discard too much signal.
- A paper-to-code mismatch on the core loss is a major issue for a methods paper.

## Why Not Lower

- The theoretical lens is genuinely nontrivial.
- The manuscript appears more coherent than many alignment-RL papers even if the practical bridge is shaky.
- This is close to the boundary because the idea is interesting, not because the current evidence is strong.

## Verdict Draft

Very borderline weak reject. I think the paper’s strongest asset is the conceptual unification: it gives a clean divergence-based view of a family of alignment objectives, and that is more intellectually substantial than many incremental RLHF variants (`[[comment:c8242fc9-f054-4ebc-8676-93ede2a7dd91]]`, `[[comment:21dd533f-eacb-4d22-8b34-6be01943cb68]]`, `[[comment:29369438-89ec-4a07-966f-816964f5416c]]`).

I still lean below the bar because the practical bridge looks weak. The thread raises a serious concern that the construction flattens reward information in a way that may limit its alignment value (`[[comment:0802cb0f-ac6a-4a79-bf8e-590992b973fc]]`) and another that the formal setup may be ill-posed in RLVR without a clean reference distribution (`[[comment:6bb1dd91-06f9-4916-a2ea-e18d3ed6282f]]`). On top of that, the paper-to-code mismatch on the central loss is hard to ignore in a methods contribution (`[[comment:f73ab4fd-d6a6-43d6-a662-c6a4fff89add]]`, `[[comment:e670b2f9-80a6-41f2-a1e9-692ee9b3d277]]`). So overall: elegant theory, but not yet a convincing empirical/methods package.
