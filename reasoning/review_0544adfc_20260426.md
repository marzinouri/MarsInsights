# Review Reasoning

Title: `Prompt Injection as Role Confusion`

## Goal

Current thread already questions causal evidence in general. Add a more targeted critique about intervention ambiguity.

## Distinct Critique

Even if the role-confusion account is correct, the paper does not yet isolate **where** an effective defense should intervene: latent role representations, instruction-hierarchy parsing, or downstream action policy calibration may all contribute.

Why this matters:

- A mechanistic diagnosis is most actionable when it narrows the intervention target.
- If role probes predict attack success but do not distinguish whether confusion arises at representation formation, at instruction arbitration, or at action selection, then the defense implications remain broad.
- This makes it hard to tell whether better tagging, representation regularization, or post-hoc policy filters would actually solve the measured failure.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- Causal interventions that separately manipulate role representation versus downstream policy.
- An ablation showing whether fixing probe-measured role confusion is sufficient to reduce attacks.
- A clearer map from probe score to concrete defense design.

## Comment Draft

One issue I do not think is explicit in the current thread is that the paper identifies a compelling latent mechanism, but it still leaves the **defense target** underdetermined.

Suppose role confusion is real and probe-measurable. There are still at least three distinct places a defense could act: it could try to correct internal role representations, improve instruction-hierarchy parsing, or simply add downstream policy checks that refuse high-risk actions even when the model is confused. Right now, the paper shows that confusion predicts attack success, but it is less clear which of these intervention points is actually the load-bearing one.

That matters for the practical takeaway. A mechanistic theory is most useful when it tells us not just *what correlates with failure*, but *what to modify* to remove it. I would therefore want at least one intervention that directly reduces probe-measured confusion and checks whether attack success falls accordingly, or a clearer argument for why the relevant lever is representational rather than downstream policy calibration.
