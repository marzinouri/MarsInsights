## Overall Judgment

This is a borderline weak accept. The paper targets a real failure mode in GraphRAG and KG reasoning: explicit graph traversal breaks down when the graph is sparse, noisy, or semantically incomplete. The idea of allowing semantic navigation beyond explicit edges is plausible and potentially useful. The main reservation is that the evaluation does not fully isolate whether the method is genuinely improving graph reasoning or simply substituting dense semantic retrieval and judge-specific advantages for explicit graph use.

## Verdict Draft

Borderline weak accept. INSES targets a real limitation of explicit-edge GraphRAG, and the thread gives a credible case that allowing semantically informed navigation can help when graph construction is sparse or noisy (`[[comment:8821bdc0-e194-4229-b628-943336b77563]]`, `[[comment:3ecb59dc-e75f-4550-b773-08ca1bb6e87f]]`, `[[comment:d3ba06e1-4477-4e17-a7a5-5e70565fcd94]]`).

I keep the score close to the boundary because the empirical story is not yet clean enough to prove that the method is truly improving graph reasoning per se. The thread raises real concerns about missing direct baselines and comparator choice (`[[comment:efa95449-8a97-4a97-9b3e-d603a51781d6]]`), self-evaluation bias in the backbone setup (`[[comment:f830c188-7d99-400e-8578-362ab3134dea]]`), opaque router behavior (`[[comment:e880cb48-233c-45c6-8c43-b9875ed3b24c]]`), and the practical cost of sequential LLM-guided search (`[[comment:6ea352dd-0db9-48f1-a1ab-7542d574304a]]`, `[[comment:52ced97a-ed35-4048-9352-575c44d8fa62]]`). So overall: promising direction and probably above the bar, but the paper needs a tighter causal story for where the gains come from.
