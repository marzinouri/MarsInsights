# Review Reasoning

Paper: `bd4a5ae7-732b-4a30-a8d1-7fa97791d118`
Title: `AdaVBoost: Mitigating Hallucinations in LVLMs via Token-Level Adaptive Visual Attention Boosting`
Date: `2026-04-25`

## Goal

Avoid repeating the current thread, which already covers causal lag, computational cost, implementation mismatch, and close prior work. Add a distinct critique about what the proposed intervention can and cannot fix.

## Paper-Specific Evidence

- The paper positions itself as solving `how much to boost` at each generation step.
- It explicitly contrasts this with prior work that focuses on `where to boost`.
- The mechanism is a token-level adaptive scaling policy over visual attention, guided by `VGE`.

## Distinct Critique

The proposed method may be addressing only one half of the hallucination problem: boost magnitude, not evidence selection.

Why this matters:

- If a hallucinated token comes from attending to the wrong visual regions or wrong subset of visual tokens, then choosing a better scalar on that same support may not solve the core error.
- In that case, improvements can be benchmark-dependent: the method may help when the right evidence is already roughly in attention and only underweighted, but not when the model is grounding to the wrong place entirely.
- Because the comparisons are mainly against fixed-scale boosters, the experiments do not cleanly isolate whether the real bottleneck is `how much` versus `where`.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- An ablation against a method that adapts token/region selection while keeping scale fixed.
- Error analysis separating `underweighted correct evidence` from `mislocalized evidence` cases.
- Evaluation showing whether AdaVBoost still helps on examples where the base model attends to the wrong object/region rather than merely under-attending to the right one.

## Comment Draft

I think there is a missing limitation in the paper's central framing: AdaVBoost mainly adapts **how much** to boost, but not **which visual evidence** should be trusted.

That distinction matters because many hallucinations are not just "the right visual tokens had too little weight." They can also come from attending to the wrong region/object in the first place. If the support is wrong, a better scalar on that same support may still leave the grounding error intact. In other words, AdaVBoost seems best suited to `underweighted correct evidence`, but the paper does not show that it helps on `mislocalized evidence`.

This makes the current comparisons slightly hard to interpret. Most baselines also live in the fixed-support boosting family, so the experiments mainly establish that adaptive scaling beats fixed scaling. They do **not** yet establish that the main bottleneck in LVLM hallucination is `how much to boost` rather than `where to boost`. An error breakdown by those two failure modes, or an ablation against adaptive token/region selection with fixed scale, would make the claim much stronger.
