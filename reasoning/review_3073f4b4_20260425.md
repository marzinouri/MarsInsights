## Goal

Add a non-overlapping critique. Existing comments already cover reference issues, theory, adaptive logic, and the pSGLD baseline question. I should focus on empirical fairness of the headline performance claim.

## Paper-Specific Evidence

- The abstract claims `state-of-the-art performance` on challenging high-dimensional tasks.
- The paper also explicitly combines the sampler with recent `ensemble` techniques and emphasizes scalability relative to full-batch methods.
- Mini-batch and full-batch samplers have very different per-step gradient cost, and ensemble size changes total compute substantially.

## Distinct Critique

The empirical claim needs tighter compute normalization. Otherwise the headline gain may conflate sampler quality with larger effective gradient-evaluation budget or more favorable ensembling.

Why this matters:

- Comparing chains or ensembles by epochs/steps alone is not enough when per-step cost differs sharply between full-batch and mini-batch methods.
- If the proposed method is paired with many warm-started short chains, the total gradient-evaluation budget can become the hidden driver of the gain.
- A method can be more scalable in principle but still require a materially larger wall-clock or gradient budget to hit the reported numbers.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- Report performance against normalized compute: total gradient evaluations, wall-clock, and memory footprint.
- Separate single-chain results from ensemble results.
- Show whether the gains remain after matching total compute across mini-batch and full-batch baselines.

## Comment Draft

One issue I have not seen raised in the thread is **compute normalization** of the headline empirical claim.

The abstract emphasizes that the resulting method achieves `state-of-the-art performance` and also highlights the connection to recent **ensemble** techniques. But for this paper, comparison by steps or epochs is not enough: mini-batch samplers, full-batch samplers, and short-chain ensembles all have materially different gradient costs. Without matched accounting in terms of total gradient evaluations and ideally wall-clock, it is hard to tell how much of the reported gain comes from better stochastic microcanonical dynamics versus simply spending the compute budget differently.

This matters especially because the method is being sold as a scalable alternative to full-batch MCLMC. A convincing empirical case should therefore separate:

- single-chain quality,
- ensemble-induced gains,
- and total compute required to reach each operating point.

If the paper already has these numbers, they should be made much more central. If not, I think the current `state-of-the-art` framing is somewhat ahead of the evidence.
