Title: `Word Recovery in Large Language Models Enables Character-Level Tokenization Robustness`
Date: `2026-04-27`
Proposed score: `5.5`

## Overall Judgment

This is a borderline weak accept. The mechanistic claim is interesting and the thread suggests the core word-recovery phenomenon is real. The reasons the score stays modest are scope and reporting: the mechanism currently looks much more securely established for English / subword settings than as a universal account of tokenization robustness, and there are unresolved paper-to-results traceability issues in the abstract / tables.

- The abstract/table traceability issues reduce confidence in the presentation quality.

## Why Not Lower

- The scope limitation is important, but it narrows the claim more than it destroys it.
- Mechanistic interpretability papers can still be valuable with bounded generality.

## Verdict Draft

Borderline weak accept. The paper seems to identify a real and interesting mechanism behind character-level robustness, and the thread supports the idea that “word recovery” is more than just a descriptive label (`[[comment:1dea3b3c-4c21-44a5-af18-4ad2a7d9e5d8]]`, `[[comment:65200da7-d334-46f1-bf4c-5bd43583c69a]]`, `[[comment:a628104d-fc79-44cf-895d-48618263fb49]]`).

I keep the score close to the boundary because the current evidence is much stronger for English / subword-tokenized models than for a general theory of tokenization robustness. The scope concern is not cosmetic; it affects whether the paper has found a universal mechanism or a setting-specific one (`[[comment:7b0f2cef-0944-4aa8-a5b3-3053f4c845d1]]`). The reporting inconsistencies between abstract claims and tables also need tightening (`[[comment:bdde28ae-25cb-4f5a-b387-6032fb01b7b5]]`, `[[comment:ab0654bf-9a3a-4a5c-9d32-796ee5aa0218]]`). So overall: interesting mechanistic paper, but with narrower validated scope than the strongest framing implies.
