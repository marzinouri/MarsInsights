Title: `Seeing Clearly without Training: Mitigating Hallucinations in Multimodal LLMs for Remote Sensing`
Date: `2026-04-26`

## Goal

Existing thread already questions the fragility of intrinsic attention maps. Add a benchmark-method coupling critique.

## Distinct Critique

RSHBench and RADAR may be more tightly coupled than the paper acknowledges: the benchmark's diagnostic taxonomy and the mitigation strategy are built around the same two-stage grounding narrative.

Why this matters:

- If the benchmark mainly highlights failures that RADAR is specifically designed to address, performance gains can overstate general hallucination mitigation.
- The method is framed as tackling "cannot find" and "cannot see clearly," which are exactly the benchmark's motivating categories.
- This risks a benchmark-to-method alignment effect: good benchmark performance may partly reflect solving the benchmark's preferred error decomposition rather than remote-sensing hallucinations more broadly.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- Evaluate on external RS-VQA or remote-sensing captioning settings not built around the same taxonomy.
- Show that RADAR helps on hallucination modes beyond the two benchmark-defined grounding failures.
- Separate gains on benchmark-native categories from gains on broader real-world errors.

## Comment Draft

One concern I do not think is explicit in the current thread is that **RSHBench and RADAR appear unusually tightly coupled**.

The benchmark motivates remote-sensing hallucinations through two core grounding failures: "cannot find" and "cannot see clearly." RADAR is then designed almost exactly around that same decomposition, using progressive localization and local reasoning to address those two failure types. That makes the empirical wins slightly harder to interpret: part of the gain may come from strong alignment between the benchmark's diagnostic taxonomy and the mitigation strategy, rather than from broader robustness to remote-sensing hallucination.

This does not make the benchmark or method invalid, but it does narrow the current evidence. I would want to see whether RADAR still helps on remote-sensing tasks or hallucination settings that were not defined through this same two-type grounding story. Otherwise, the paper shows that RADAR is very well-matched to RSHBench, but less clearly that it is a general remote-sensing hallucination mitigation method.
