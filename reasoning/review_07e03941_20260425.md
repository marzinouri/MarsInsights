# Review Reasoning

- Paper ID: `07e03941-6b21-4453-a82f-40df30c4752d`
- Title: `Missing-Data-Induced Phase Transitions in Spectral PLS for Multimodal Learning`
- Date: `2026-04-25`

## Core Claim

- Under dual entry-wise MCAR missingness in a proportional high-dimensional spiked model, spectral PLS behaves like a spiked rectangular random matrix with effective signal attenuation by `sqrt(rho)`.
- This yields a sharp BBP-style recovery threshold plus closed-form overlap predictions.

## Main Concerns

- The paper is strongest as a theory paper, but the practical story is overstated.
- The central theorem is built on a narrow model class: rank-1 signal, whitened design, Gaussian noise, independent MCAR masks, and proportional asymptotics.
- The semi-synthetic biological validation is much less realistic than it first appears. The authors extract PLS directions from real data, then regenerate responses with controlled Gaussian noise and synthetic masking. This tests whether the theorem survives more realistic covariance geometry, but it does not show that real multimodal missingness behaves the same way.

## Methodological Weaknesses

- The main theorem is replica-symmetric rather than fully rigorous. The appendix is candid about this, but the theorem should still be treated as a strong prediction with empirical support, not as a settled mathematical characterization.
- The semi-synthetic construction may be especially favorable to spectral PLS, because the “biological” signal directions are themselves extracted by leading PLS-SVD and then used to generate a rank-1 response.
- The robustness appendix is useful, but it remains synthetic throughout, including the MAR and imputation checks. That does not close the gap to real missingness mechanisms in applied multimodal datasets.

## Logical Gaps

- The conclusion sometimes reads as if missingness primarily acts through simple signal attenuation in multimodal learning more generally. What the paper really establishes is that this is true in the authors' stylized model family, and approximately true in their controlled synthetic robustness tests.
- The split-half stability diagnostic is plausible, but it is validated inside the same stylized / semi-synthetic world. That is not yet enough to establish strong practical utility.
- The appendix result that missing-as-zero normalization is effectively optimal under MCAR is interesting, but it should not be read as evidence against richer missing-data methods in realistic MAR/MNAR regimes.

## Missing Evidence

- Real downstream validation where one actually compares PLS-SVD, imputation-based methods, and probabilistic multi-view models on naturally missing multimodal data.
- Multi-spike analysis. Many real multimodal problems care about more than the top shared factor.
- Sensitivity to whitening and preprocessing choices, which are built into both the theorem and the semi-synthetic protocol.

## Bottom Line

- I find the theoretical direction interesting and likely publishable on those grounds.
- But the practical message needs tighter calibration: the current evidence supports a sharp asymptotic insight for a clean model much more strongly than it supports a broad characterization of real multimodal learning under missing data.
