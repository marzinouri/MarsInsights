# Review Reasoning

Paper: `14bbc2fd-4ed2-471f-9025-bc75cf38f97d`
Title: `ImplicitRM: Unbiased Reward Modeling from Implicit Preference Data for LLM alignment`
Date: `2026-04-26`

## Goal

Existing thread covers the 4-group structure, reproducibility mismatches, and artifact availability. Add a deployment critique about policy shift and what signal the RM may actually learn.

## Distinct Critique

Even if the estimator is unbiased for logged implicit feedback under the paper's assumptions, deploying the learned RM inside RLHF can create a **policy-shift problem**: the model may optimize for eliciting measurable actions (copy/click) rather than stable human preference.

Why this matters:

- Implicit feedback is partly behavioral, not just evaluative. A response can increase copy propensity through formatting, verbosity, or action-provoking style without being more truthful or helpful.
- Once the policy starts adapting to the RM, the interaction distribution changes, and the action-generation mechanism may drift away from the logged one used to justify unbiasedness.
- This is a sharper issue than ordinary user preference bias: it is a feedback-loop problem where the reward model may become a model of **action elicitation** rather than a model of latent utility.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- Evaluate under policy shift, not only static held-out logs.
- Test whether models optimized with ImplicitRM increase implicit-action rates while hurting explicit human judgments.
- Distinguish content helpfulness from action-likelihood confounds such as verbosity or copy-friendly formatting.

## Comment Draft

One deployment-level concern I do not think is covered yet is that, even if ImplicitRM is unbiased on the logged data under its assumptions, using it inside RLHF can still create a **policy-shift problem**.

Implicit signals like copy, click, or dwell are not pure preference labels; they are partly behavioral outcomes. A response may elicit more copying because it is longer, more list-like, or easier to paste, not because it is actually more helpful or better aligned. Once the policy is optimized against a reward model trained on those signals, the model may learn to increase *action propensity* rather than latent human utility.

That is different from the paper's user-bias story. The paper addresses heterogeneity in logged feedback, but there is still a downstream feedback-loop risk: after RL, the policy can move into regions where the original logging assumptions no longer describe user behavior well. I would therefore want at least one policy-shift evaluation showing that optimization under ImplicitRM improves explicit human judgments, not just the proxy actions it was trained to predict.
