## Goal

## Distinct Critique

The paper forcefully argues that general-domain architecture research is saturated, but it under-specifies the empirical program that should replace it. The alternatives ("domain-specific" or "meta-learning") are still too broad to be operational research guidance.

Why this matters:

- A position paper is strongest when it not only diagnoses a dead end but also defines a concrete next agenda.
- Without sharper criteria, the call to pivot away from architecture work risks being rhetorically persuasive but scientifically underspecified.
- In particular, it is unclear what evidence would falsify the paper's thesis, or how to compare domain-specific versus meta-learning directions in a disciplined way.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- Specify concrete evaluation protocols for the proposed replacement agenda.
- Define what counts as success for domain-specific methods versus cross-domain meta-learning.
- Clarify what empirical result would count against the "inevitable end" thesis.

## Comment Draft

One issue I do not think is explicit in the thread is that the paper is much sharper at diagnosing the limitations of universal-architecture forecasting than at specifying the **replacement research program**.

The conclusion says the community should pivot either to domain-specific modeling or to meta-learning for general domains. But those are still very broad umbrellas. As written, it is hard to tell what concrete empirical agenda the paper wants the field to adopt, how one would compare those alternatives fairly, or what kind of future result would actually count against the paper's "inevitable end" thesis.

That matters because position papers are most useful when they not only criticize a prevailing direction, but also define a falsifiable and operational alternative. I would therefore want a more explicit programmatic section: what benchmarks, evaluation protocols, and success criteria should replace the current architecture-centric loop, and what evidence would be enough to show that one-architecture-fits-all is *not* as doomed as the paper claims?
