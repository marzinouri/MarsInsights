# Review Reasoning

- Paper ID: `ea4ff055-3837-4e12-bd02-7a2037a8b96e`
- Title: `When Shared Knowledge Hurts: Spectral Over-Accumulation in Model Merging`
- Date: `2026-04-26`

## Core Claim

- The paper argues that naive merging over-counts aligned shared directions, inflating top singular values and hurting merged performance.
- It proposes Singular Value Calibration (SVC), a data-free post-processing step that keeps singular directions fixed and rescales singular values according to estimated overlap.

## Main Concerns

- The central mechanism is plausible, but the paper may be attributing too much of merged-performance degradation to singular-value inflation alone.
- Because SVC only rescales singular values while keeping directions fixed, it cannot repair directional misalignment inside a shared subspace. If the real problem is not just excessive magnitude but the wrong basis/direction mix, SVC can only partially address it.
- The method also risks suppressing genuinely useful shared structure, not just harmful over-counting. Shared directions are often exactly what one wants transfer to preserve in model merging.

## Methodological Weaknesses

- The paper keeps the global update scale `lambda = 1` throughout experiments and explicitly notes that jointly tuning `lambda` with SVC could yield further gains. This creates an attribution problem: some gains credited to subspace-wise calibration may partly reflect compensation for a globally suboptimal merge scale.
- The mechanism is derived most transparently for additive merging, then argued to generalize. That is reasonable, but the strongest theoretical support still tracks the simplest setting more closely than the broader empirical claim across many merging methods.
- The benchmark story is broad, but the paper still does not cleanly separate three possible effects: conflict reduction, scale correction, and true shared-knowledge over-counting. These can all improve merged performance and are not fully disentangled experimentally.

## Logical Gaps

- The paper presents “shared knowledge hurts” as a distinct failure mode, but the evidence currently supports a softer statement: excessive accumulation of some shared directions can hurt under the evaluated merge procedures. That is weaker than treating shared knowledge itself as the main culprit.
- Since SVC leaves directions unchanged, any case where bad performance comes from directional incompatibility rather than mere spectral inflation should remain unresolved. This limits how general the paper's causal story can be.

## Missing Evidence

- Stronger ablations that jointly tune the global merge coefficient `lambda` with and without SVC.
- Cases designed to separate magnitude over-accumulation from directional misalignment within shared subspaces.
- Evidence that SVC remains beneficial when useful shared structure is dominant and should arguably be preserved rather than suppressed.

## Bottom Line

- I find the “over-accumulated shared directions” hypothesis credible and worth publishing.
- But the current evidence is stronger for “SVC is a useful scale-calibration heuristic for merged spectra” than for the broader claim that spectral over-accumulation is the dominant missing explanation for merging failures.
