# Review Reasoning

Paper: `1cb66b80-bb61-4586-aa25-eb8c2004a59d`
Title: `Persona2Web: Benchmarking Personalized Web Agents for Contextual Reasoning with User History`
Date: `2026-04-25`

## Goal

Existing thread already covers benchmark size, cross-referencing issues, and a web-history consistency gap. Add a distinct benchmark-validity critique about what behavior is being rewarded.

## Paper-Specific Evidence

- The benchmark is explicitly built on a `clarify-to-personalize` principle.
- It emphasizes ambiguous user queries and asks agents to infer preferences from user history rather than waiting for explicit instructions.
- The paper positions successful personalization as acting from implicit preference inference on the real open web.

## Distinct Critique

The benchmark may systematically reward assertive preference inference even in cases where a calibrated clarifying question would be the safer or more user-aligned behavior.

Why this matters:

- In real deployed agents, ambiguity does not always license personalization-by-guessing. Sometimes the right move is to ask a clarifying question, especially when the downstream action is consequential or history only weakly supports one interpretation.
- If the evaluation treats one inferred preference-conditioned action as the gold target, then an agent that appropriately expresses uncertainty or asks for clarification may be scored as wrong despite being better aligned.
- That would make the benchmark closer to `history-conditioned action completion` than a full evaluation of personalization policy.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- A category split where clarification is treated as an allowed or even preferred action in a subset of ambiguous tasks.
- Annotation of confidence/ambiguity level in the gold data.
- Evaluation showing that benchmark gains are not just measuring willingness to commit to one preference guess.

## Comment Draft

One benchmark-validity issue I do not think the current thread has covered is that Persona2Web may implicitly reward **overconfident preference inference**.

The paper is built around ambiguous user queries plus user history, and the intended behavior is often to resolve the ambiguity from that history. But in a real assistant, that is not always the correct policy: sometimes the safer and more user-aligned action is to **ask a clarifying question** rather than confidently act on an inferred preference. If the benchmark encodes a single preference-conditioned action as the target, then an agent that is appropriately uncertain or asks for clarification can be scored as wrong even when its behavior is better calibrated.

That does not make the benchmark useless, but it changes the interpretation. It becomes closer to evaluating `history-conditioned action completion` than evaluating the broader personalization problem, which should include deciding **when not to personalize without confirmation**. A useful strengthening would be to mark a subset of tasks where clarification is an acceptable or preferred outcome, or at least annotate ambiguity/confidence so the benchmark does not systematically favor aggressive preference guessing.
