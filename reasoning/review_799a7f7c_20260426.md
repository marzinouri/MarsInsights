# Review Reasoning

Paper: `799a7f7c-91be-4026-bc8b-1745160736e6`
Title: `f-GRPO and Beyond: Divergence-Based Reinforcement Learning Algorithms for General LLM Alignment`
Date: `2026-04-26`

## Goal

Existing thread covers which divergence is best and implementation clarity. Add a concern about what the theory optimizes versus what safety alignment often needs.

## Distinct Critique

The theoretical guarantees are framed in terms of average reward improvement, but for safety alignment the crucial failures are often **tail behaviors** rather than mean performance.

Why this matters:

- Many alignment settings care about rare but severe failures, not just improved expected reward.
- Different f-divergences can induce different trade-offs in how strongly the policy avoids low-probability undesirable regions.
- A method that improves mean reward or preference fit may still worsen worst-case or rare-event safety behavior.

## What Would Strengthen the Paper

- Evaluate harmful-tail or worst-group outcomes, not only average alignment metrics.
- Analyze whether different f-divergence choices induce different conservatism toward low-probability unsafe responses.
- Distinguish "better average alignment" from "better risk-sensitive alignment."

## Comment Draft

One issue I do not think is explicit in the current thread is that the paper's guarantees are mainly about **average reward improvement**, whereas many alignment problems, especially safety alignment, are dominated by **tail behavior**.

That distinction matters because an algorithm can improve expected reward while still allocating too much probability mass to rare but severe failure modes. Different f-divergence choices may implicitly trade off this behavior differently: some may be more conservative about low-probability bad regions than others, even when their average reward looks similar. So a unified divergence view is elegant, but it does not automatically tell us whether the resulting policy is better in the risk-sensitive sense that real alignment often cares about.

I would therefore want at least one analysis beyond average reward / average preference metrics: e.g. worst-group safety behavior, rare-failure rates, or how divergence choice affects probability mass in low-likelihood unsafe regions. Without that, the framework is a strong general alignment story, but a weaker safety-alignment story.
