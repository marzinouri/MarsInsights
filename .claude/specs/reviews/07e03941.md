# Review Target

- Paper ID: `07e03941-6b21-4453-a82f-40df30c4752d`
- Title: `Missing-Data-Induced Phase Transitions in Spectral PLS for Multimodal Learning`
- Status when selected: `in_review`
- Comment count when selected: `0`

## Core Claim
- Under dual entry-wise MCAR missingness in a proportional high-dimensional spiked model, spectral PLS behaves like a spiked rectangular random matrix with effective signal attenuation by `sqrt(rho)`.
- This yields a sharp BBP-style recovery threshold and closed-form asymptotic overlap predictions for the leading singular vectors.

## Methodological Weaknesses
- The main theory is built on a narrow model class: single-spike, whitened design, Gaussian noise, independent entry-wise MCAR masks, and proportional asymptotics. That is analytically clean but substantially simpler than real multimodal missing-data settings.
- The semi-synthetic biological validation is not truly observational validation. The authors extract signal directions from real data, then regenerate outcomes with controlled Gaussian noise and synthetic masking. That mainly tests whether their theorem survives a more realistic covariance geometry, not whether it explains actual multimodal biological missingness.
- The paper relies on replica-symmetric calculations for its central theorem rather than a fully rigorous proof. They acknowledge this in the appendix, but the main result is still less mathematically secure than the presentation may initially suggest.
- The robustness appendix is useful, but it does not solve the core external-validity issue: even the MAR, non-Gaussian, and imputation comparisons remain fully synthetic and still inherit the same rank-1 whitened latent construction.

## Logical Gaps
- The conclusion sometimes reads stronger than the evidence supports. The paper shows that MCAR missingness behaves like signal attenuation in this model family; it does not show that real multimodal missingness “primarily” acts this way in general.
- The practical diagnostic based on split-half stability is plausible, but it is validated in the same stylized/semi-synthetic framework as the main theory. That is not enough to establish field-ready usefulness.
- The appendix argues that simple missing-as-zero normalization is effectively optimal under MCAR, but this does not extend to MAR/MNAR settings where the strongest practical need for missing-data methods often arises.
- The semi-synthetic protocol may be especially favorable to the proposed story because the biological directions are themselves extracted by leading PLS-SVD and then used to generate a rank-1 response. That makes it less surprising that spectral PLS recovers them with theory-like behavior.

## Novelty & Significance
- The paper appears genuinely novel in bringing phase-transition analysis to dual-masked spectral PLS, extending the spirit of prior PCA-with-missing-data theory to a two-view setting.
- The significance is mainly theoretical. The work is less convincing as a practical multimodal-learning paper because the real-data component is still heavily synthetic.
- The most convincing significance claim is therefore “new asymptotic insight about a classical estimator,” not “actionable characterization of real multimodal learning under missing data” in a broad applied sense.

## Missing Evidence
- Missing validation on real downstream tasks where practitioners actually choose between PLS-SVD, imputation-based methods, and probabilistic two-view models under naturally occurring missingness.
- Missing multi-spike analyses. Many multimodal settings care about more than the top shared factor, and behavior beyond rank-1 could differ materially.
- Missing stronger non-MCAR evaluation in the main text. The appendix includes MAR robustness experiments, but those remain synthetic and do not rescue the gap to real missingness mechanisms.
- Missing sensitivity analysis to whitening and preprocessing choices. Because the theorem is stated for whitened designs and the semi-synthetic pipeline enforces this structure, it is unclear how fragile the conclusions are to preprocessing mismatches common in practice.

## Failure Cases & Risks
- The theorem may be brittle outside the rank-1 Gaussian world: correlated masks, feature-dependent missingness, heavy-tailed latent structure, or multiple shared components could all shift the threshold substantially.
- Because the semi-synthetic protocol uses leading PLS directions extracted from the same biological datasets, it risks baking in a spectral structure especially favorable to spectral PLS.
- The practical recommendation to rely on split-half stability could mislead users in smaller, noisier, or more heterogeneous datasets where unstable preprocessing dominates the signal/noise story.

## External Validation
- The paper is well aligned with prior primary-source theory on missing-data PCA, especially the ICML 2019 result that missingness reduces effective signal-to-noise rather than merely shrinking sample size. This strengthens the conceptual plausibility of the paper’s main message.
- At the same time, existing statistical work on missing data emphasizes MCAR/MAR/MNAR distinctions and model-based estimation under missingness. That literature limits how far one should extrapolate this paper’s “simple attenuation” story.
- The appendix’s comparison against EM-PLS and iterative SVD is only under the authors’ controlled MCAR generative model. That is useful as an internal consistency check, but it is not decisive evidence against richer missing-data methods in realistic applied pipelines.

## Clarity Issues
- The paper is clearly written, but the headline practical relevance can be overstated because the semi-synthetic experiments look more realistic than they actually are.
- The main text would benefit from a more explicit front-loaded caveat that the theorem is replica-based and rank-1, with real-data results serving as stress tests of geometry rather than genuine observational validation.
