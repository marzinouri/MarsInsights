# Review Target

- Paper ID: `0bb9fe86-b711-4b1f-bec5-035ec976f497`
- Title: `Simple Baselines are Competitive with Code Evolution`
- Status when selected: `in_review`
- Comment count when selected: `0`

## Core Claim
- Simple baselines for code evolution, especially IID random sampling and sequential conditioned sampling, match or beat more elaborate pipelines across three domains.
- The paper further claims that search-space design and evaluation protocol matter more than sophisticated evolutionary machinery.

## Methodological Weaknesses
- The evidence base is thin relative to the strength of the general claim. Several central comparisons rely on a single expensive run of the stronger baseline system per problem/domain, which makes variance and unlucky seeds hard to separate from genuine method differences.
- The paper explicitly states that only a single ShinkaEvolve run is used per math problem because the experiments are expensive, and that the overall field lacks standardization. That makes the headline conclusion directionally interesting, but not well identified enough for a strong cross-domain empirical claim.
- The paper argues for fairness, but some comparisons still depend on prompt adaptation and implementation choices made by the authors. That leaves room for “baseline optimization by authorship” rather than a neutral comparison.
- In the scaffold experiments, ADAS is manually adapted from GPQA-style prompts to AIME and the authors report that running ADAS with Gemini performed worse. That makes the “same setup” story less clean than the main narrative suggests.
- In the agentic-scaffold setting, the paper shows heavy stochasticity, but that also weakens its own headline claim: if selection noise dominates, then “method A beats method B” becomes underidentified unless all methods are re-run many times under the same protocol.
- The MLE-bench comparison is limited to a 10-competition subset and one resource envelope. That is a reasonable pilot, but too small to support broad claims about code evolution pipelines in time-limited ML competitions.
- The machine-learning-competition comparison is against AIDE, which the paper itself notes is simpler than some other code-evolution systems. So even a clean win there would not establish that simple baselines beat the best sophisticated pipelines.

## Logical Gaps
- The jump from “simple baselines are competitive on these tasks” to “sophisticated pipelines are often redundant” is too strong. Competitive performance does not imply the extra machinery is useless; it may only imply current benchmarks are not sensitive enough.
- The mathematical-bounds section partly changes the problem formulation and then uses the resulting larger gain to argue that search-space design dominates search strategy. That is plausible, but it conflates “verifier/search-space quality matters” with “search algorithm choice is secondary” without a clean causal decomposition.
- The paper sometimes slides between criticizing evaluation methodology in prior work and establishing superiority of its own baselines. Those are different claims and the second is less firmly supported than the first.

## Novelty & Significance
- The strongest contribution is methodological skepticism and benchmark hygiene, not a new search method. That is useful, but the headline can overstate algorithmic novelty.
- The baselines themselves are not especially surprising conceptually; the real contribution is the cross-domain empirical demonstration and the argument that prior comparisons were weak.

## Missing Evidence
- Missing multi-seed reruns of the stronger comparator systems under exactly the same prompts, models, and budgets.
- Missing ablations isolating which parts of the proposed sequential conditioned sampling baseline matter most.
- Missing sensitivity analysis to model family. Much of the story may depend on Gemini 2.5 Pro being strong enough that search strategy differences shrink.
- Missing evaluation on domains where long-horizon memory, explicit selection pressure, or structured mutation should matter more than one-shot/randomized sampling.
- Missing a more neutral benchmark protocol where each method is run by authors or maintainers closest to that method, to reduce implementation-quality confounding.

## Failure Cases & Risks
- The paper may be correct only in “easy-to-hit” search spaces where base models already generate many workable candidates; it may fail on domains requiring deep iterative repair or nontrivial stateful exploration.
- The negative-result framing could age badly if stronger code agents or better evaluators make sophisticated search materially more useful.

## External Validation
- The paper’s positioning against stronger code-evolution narratives is grounded in recent agent papers such as AlphaEvolve, AIDE, and ShinkaEvolve, but those systems are heterogeneous and often bundle prompt engineering, verifier design, and search. That supports the paper’s critique of confounded comparisons more than it supports any universal anti-evolution conclusion.
- Prior work on AlphaEvolve emphasizes discovery wins, not always tightly controlled ablations of search components. That is consistent with the paper’s criticism, but does not by itself prove the simple baselines are broadly sufficient.
- Earlier systems such as FunSearch and AlphaCode already contain components that look closer to guided sampling/search than to rich evolutionary machinery. That weakens any attempt to cast the paper’s baselines as conceptually disruptive rather than a careful reminder that older, simpler ideas remain strong.

## Clarity Issues
- The paper is generally clear, but the exact fairness envelope varies by domain and is easy to lose track of. A tighter table of “same prompt / same model / same budget / same verifier / tuned by whom” would make the claims easier to audit.
- The discussion occasionally mixes “baseline competitiveness,” “evaluation unreliability,” and “search-space dominance” into one narrative, when these should be separated more explicitly.
